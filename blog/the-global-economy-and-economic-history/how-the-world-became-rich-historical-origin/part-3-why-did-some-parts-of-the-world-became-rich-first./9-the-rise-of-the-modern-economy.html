Cotton:
...

  • Demand for slaves rosed
    • Slave produced cotton was highly profitable.
  • Booming cotton trade drove the center of slavery south and west.
  • Slave-produced cotton was highly profitable
    • Played a central role on Southern society.
  • Southern elites held their wealth primarily in the form of slaves

Removing salvery (P 191)
...

  • Removing slavery did not mean the end of cotton production; manufacturers would have purchased cotton from non-slave sources
  • Cotton production could have expanded through small-scale farming in the medium-run (Wright, 2020)
  • Southern elites would have been poorer without slavery, but the impact on textile production would likely be a temporary disruption
  • During the War of 1812, when Britain was briefly cut off from American cotton imports, the price of cotton rose less than in the 1860s, suggesting less dependence in the earlier period (Clegg, 2015, p. 296)
  • In the Northeast, the technologies of the First Industrial Revolution spread earliest, leading to a 100-fold expansion of industrial production between 1790 and 1850
  • The growth in industrial production in the Northeast was facilitated by the rapid spread of ideas from Britain, aided by a shared Anglo culture between Britain and the newly independent US

Allen's Argument on Relative Factor Prices (p 192)
...

  • Real Wages in America:

    • High real wages in America before the Revolution, possibly exceeding those in Britain (Lindert and Williamson, 2016).
    • Cheap energy, abundant water power, and natural resources like timber created a strong incentive for labor-saving innovation.

  • Labor-Saving Innovations:

    • Examples include Oliver Evans' automatic flour mill (1782) and Whitney's cotton gin.
    • Allen (2011a, p. 83) emphasizes that the success of the American economy depended on inventive engineering across industries, driven by the high cost of labor.

  • Changing Relative Prices After 1880:

    • Until 1880, the relative price of unskilled labor compared to energy and capital was not higher in the US than in Britain.
    • After 1880, increased incentives to invent higher productivity technology led to an American lead, with a significant rise in the incentives to use more power per worker (Allen, 2014, p. 332).

Precondition: Limited and Representative Government
...

  • Government Structure in the US and Britain:
    • Limited and representative government in both the US and Britain, though not universal in either case.
    • In Britain, crucial for public good provision, particularly transport networks.
    • In the US, significant due to the Interstate Commerce Clause preventing state laws hindering inter-regional trade and prohibiting transport monopolies.

Impact on Transport Infrastructure: (P 193)
...

  • Early 19th-century improvements in transport infrastructure, highlighted by the completion of the Erie Canal in 1825 connecting New York City to the Great Lakes.
  • The Interstate Commerce Clause played a role in striking down state attempts to grant monopolies, like Robert Fulton's steamboat monopoly in New York State in 1824.
  • Large Domestic Markets and Integration:(p 193)
    • Improvements in transport infrastructure facilitated inter-regional trade, leading to greater specialization and the rise of an integrated domestic market for manufactured goods (North, 1966).
    • Large domestic markets identified as a precondition in Chapter 7. The availability of such a market occurred after the connection of different regions in the US.
  • Largest Investments in Railroads:
    • Funded with both public and private funds.
    • Recent findings suggest an enormous impact on US economic growth, particularly through improved market access (Donaldson and Hornbeck, 2016).
  • Significant Contribution to Economic Growth:
    • By 1890, US aggregate productivity would have been some 25% lower without railroads (Hornbeck and Rotemberg, 2021).
    • Railroads played a crucial role in integrating the US into a large and homogeneous market, a key precondition for the rise of American manufacturing.

Role of Political Institutions in Education Investment
...

  • Investment in Education:
    • In contrast to Britain, the US invested in formal public education.
    • Local, municipal-level investment was predominant, influenced by decentralized, egalitarian, and democratic political institutions in Colonial America, especially in the Northeast.
  • Regional Disparities in Education:
    • In the South, large-scale landlords had an incentive to keep their labor force uneducated, as literacy held little value in farming.
    • Northern states, with a more industrial focus, had relatively high literacy rates and basic education.
  • Evolution of Literacy Rates:(P 193)
    • Literacy rates stagnated in the mid-19th century with industrialization and rapid immigration.
    • The early 20th century witnessed a dramatic expansion in secondary education through the high school movement (Goldin and Katz, 2008).
    • The rise in human capital in the 20th century, particularly through education, was central to American growth, termed the "human capital century" (Goldin, 2001).

American Culture's Economic Impact (p 194)
...

  • Calvinism and Benjamin Franklin:
    • Franklin represents Calvinism's impact; weak evidence for a Protestant work ethic (Appleby, 2010).
  • No Hereditary Aristocracy:
    • Absence allows unrestricted commerce; entrepreneurs face no social hindrance (Appleby, 2010).
  • Frontier Individualism:
    • Uncertain impact on industrialization; reflects a broader belief in economic improvement.

Immigration's Economic Role
...

  • Population Growth and Immigration:
    • US population grew from <4 million in 1790 to >76 million by 1900 (Figure 9.6).
  • Economic Implications of Immigration:
    • Market Expansion: Immigration expands domestic markets by the 1850s (Sequeira, Nunn, Qian, 2020).
    • Workforce Boost: Immigrants crucial for making the US a major manufacturing center.
    • Human Capital Contribution: Immigrants bring human capital, fostering American inventiveness.
  • Long-Term Impact of Immigration:
    • Counties with more immigration show higher innovation, manufacturing, and agricultural productivity (Sequeira, Nunn, Qian, 2020).
    • These counties have higher incomes, less poverty, less unemployment, more urbanization, and better education.

Empirical Insights into Pre-Soviet Russian Economy
...

  • Mid-19th Century:
    • Imperial Russia: closed, agrarian economy.
    • Modernizing reforms by late 19th century led to late industrialization.
    • By early 20th century, one of the fastest growing economies globally (Gregory, 1994).
  • Agrarian Dominance:
    • Agriculture constituted almost 60% of the economy in 1913 (Gregory, 2004b, p. 185).
    • Inefficient serf-based agriculture hindered productivity even after serfdom abolition in 1861 (Markevich and Zhuravskaya, 2018).
  • Reforms and Growth Potential:
    • Stolypin Reforms of 1906 and 1911 made peasants residual claimants, unlocking agricultural productivity (CastaÃ±eda Dower and Markevich, 2018).
  • Rapid Growth and Collapse:
    • Rapid growth ended with the collapse after World War I, Russian Revolution, and Civil War.

Lenin's War Communism and Economic Shift (pg 196)
...

  • War Communism:
    • Lenin's policy abolished markets and private property, leading to famine.
  • Post-World War I Recovery:
    • Lenin's New Economic Policy revived market activity.

Stalin's Acceleration of Industrialization
...

  • Soviet Industrialization:
    • Stalin accelerated industrialization through collectivization and Five-Year Plans.
    • Human cost: Collectivization led to mass famine; 5.5-10.8 million deaths (Markevich, Naumenko, and Qian, 2021; Naumenko, 2021).

Initial Successes and Public Goods
...

  • Post-War Soviet Industrialization:
    • Provided an alternative path for developing countries in the post-war period.
  • Soviet Achievements:
    • Offered basic public goods: excellent technical education, essential healthcare.
    • Eradicated smallpox and diseases earlier than the US (Troesken, 2015).

Challenges and Inefficiencies
...

  • Unsustainable Growth:
    • Rapid industrialization couldn't be sustained; inefficiencies and wastage were legendary (Gregory and Harrison, 2005).
    • Centralization in the hands of a dictator (Stalin) led to chaos and trivial decision-making.
  • Corruption and Rent-Seeking:
    • Endemic corruption and rent-seeking (Anderson and Boettke, 1997; Boettke, 2001).

Economic Value and Growth Failures
...

  • Consumer Goods Struggle:
    • Soviet economy struggled to provide basic consumer goods.
    • Elites had access to Western products, while ordinary citizens were denied.
  • Dependence on Exports:
    • Depended heavily on oil and natural gas exports.
    • The collapse of oil prices in the 1980s pressured reforms and led to the Soviet collapse.

Lessons from Soviet Industrialization
...

  • Incompatibility of Communism with Growth:
    • Communism failed to implement its original economic blueprint, relying on black markets.
    • Inability to suppress the profit motive led to a sclerotic economy.
  • Autocratic Government's Impact:
    • Autocratic government can stifle long-run growth.
    • Soviet case serves as a reminder of the economic consequences of unchecked political power.
